Chatuchak, November 2024

November 10, 2024 Chatuchak Weekend Market · Bangkok

I was done. Four hours into Chatuchak on a Sunday in November, the kind of heat that makes the air taste like dust and fried garlic, and I had nothing.

To be fair, nothing good — just the usual: faded band tees from bands that broke up a decade ago, tourist rayon in colors no one has worn since 1997, a scattering of 200-baht牛仔裤 that smelled like they'd been stored in a flooded basement. I'd started at section 6, worked my way through 7, 8, 26, zigzagged up toward 34, and by noon I was carrying a plastic bag with exactly one item: an unremarkable navy button-down, 150 baht, from a stack where the owner was napping in a lawn chair.

Then, between sections 5 and 6, there's this narrow corridor of folding tables that nobody seems to own. Just tables shoved together with stuff piled on them — damaged stock, overstock, returns from some warehouse somewhere. Most of it is junk. But I stopped because I saw a skull.

Not a real skull. A print. A Glad News hoodie, navy blue, screen-printed in white on the chest. Face-down on the table, half-covered under a pile of knockoff Nike socks. I picked it up and almost put it back, because I thought it was another of those ironic graphic hoodies that tourists buy and abandon. But the weight was wrong. Too heavy. This wasn't 200-baht garbage.

It was 100% cotton, thick, ring-spun. The kind of heavy cotton that doesn't exist anymore, the kind that feels like it was made in a different era. And the print — the skull was detailed, not some mass-produced vector. It had texture. I turned it over and saw the neck label: Glad News, est. 1988, Osaka, Japan. A Japanese streetwear brand from the late 90s, the real deal, the kind that barely exists anymore outside of depachika and a few select shops in Harajuku.

I didn't haggle. I paid the 300 baht the old woman behind the table asked for and left. By that point the heat had gotten to where I couldn't think straight, so I stopped at a stall selling coconut juice and sat in the shade for twenty minutes, just holding the bag, trying to process what had just happened.

That's the thing about Chatuchak. You can go eight hours with nothing, and then one item, one single piece on one table in one corridor, makes the whole day worth it.
