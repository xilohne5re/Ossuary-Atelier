## WHAT THIS DOCUMENT IS

This is the complete reference guide for building, running, and expanding
the Ossuary Atelier blog — called "The Osteology." If you are the original
author returning after a break, a collaborator helping build a feature,
or a developer taking over any part of this project, start here.

Everything you need to understand the blog's purpose, structure, technical
implementation, and editorial voice is in this document. Read it fully
before touching any code.

## THE CONCEPT IN ONE PARAGRAPH

The Osteology is the editorial and community layer of Ossuary Atelier —
a thrift and creative brand based in Phuket, Thailand. The main website
(ossuaryatelier.github.io) sells curated secondhand clothing and Archive
pieces with QR-verified story pages. The blog is where the brand's
intellectual and personal life lives: sourcing journals, thrift shop guides,
archive deep-dives, digital art, and creative commentary. It serves four
simultaneous purposes: SEO engine, brand voice platform, community space,
and creative outlet for the founder. It is written in a mix of first-person
personal voice (for item stories, sourcing journals) and first-person brand
voice (for guides, partnerships, and broader editorial).

---

## THE NAME

**The Osteology**

Osteology is the scientific study of bones — their structure, history,
and what they reveal about the lives they came from. An ossuary is a
vessel that houses bones. The blog studies the things the ossuary holds:
the garments, the objects, the shops, the culture behind them.
The name is deliberately academic-sounding in a brand that is
deliberately gothic-editorial. It works because it is specific,
unusual, and internally consistent with the brand's whole identity.

Full blog title: **Ossuary Atelier: The Osteology**
URL path: /blog/
Short reference: The Osteology

## BLOG SECTIONS — THE THREE CONTENT TYPES

The Osteology is divided into three named content types.
Each has its own label, purpose, voice, and SEO function.
They all live in the same /blog/ directory and appear in
the same index grid, but are labelled and filterable.

---

### SECTION 1: CIRCUIT
**Subtitle: The Thrift Circuit**
**Status: BUILD FIRST — launch target May 1, 2026**

What it is: A recurring series of thrift shop guides,
structured as a hub-and-spoke system. The hub is a single
comprehensive guide page (/blog/circuit/index.html) that
maps all featured shops and links out to individual shop
pages. Each shop gets its own dedicated page with a full
write-up, photos, what to expect, best days to visit,
and what kinds of finds it produces.

Why it comes first: "Best thrift shops Phuket" and related
searches are real, low-competition, high-intent keywords.
This content attracts exactly the right audience — people
interested in secondhand fashion in Thailand — and establishes
OA as a local authority before the brand has any sales history
to lean on. Shop owners who are featured have an incentive
to share it, bringing their own audience to OA's website.

Geographic scope at launch: Phuket only.
Expansion plan: Add Bangkok, Chiang Mai as author visits.
Eventually: Southeast Asia secondhand scene broadly.

Hub page (/blog/circuit/index.html) contains:
- Intro to what the Circuit is and how it works
- Map embed (Google Maps iframe, free) showing shop locations
- Grid of shop cards, each linking to individual shop page
- Author note about sourcing philosophy
- Regular update date visible (shows it's maintained)

Individual shop page (/blog/circuit/[shop-name].html) contains:
- Shop name, location, opening hours (updated regularly)
- How the author found it / first visit story
- What the shop specialises in (clothes, homeware, mixed)
- Quality of finds (honest — some shops are better than others)
- Best time/day to visit based on restocking patterns
- Price range
- 2-3 photos
- "Items we've found here" — links to any OA items sourced there
- Comment section via Giscus (see Technical section)

Voice for Circuit: First person as brand.
"Ossuary Atelier sources from across Phuket. Here is what we know."
Professional, structured, trustworthy. Think editorial travel guide
meets thrift bible.

Target posts for launch (by May 1):
- /blog/circuit/index.html — the hub, comprehensive
- /blog/circuit/waanwaal-phuket.html — first shop (ITEM-005 was found here)
- /blog/circuit/o-wa-second-hand-phuket.html — second shop (ITEM-006 found here)

### SECTION 2: FIELD NOTES
**Subtitle: Field Notes**
**Status: BUILD SECOND — first post by May 1, expand through May/June**

What it is: Personal sourcing journals. Short, specific, first person.
Written the same day or within 48 hours of a sourcing trip while
the details are still specific. These are not polished essays —
they are honest field reports. What was found, what wasn't,
what the light looked like in the shop at 9am, what the stall
owner said, why something got put back.

Why it matters: This content is the authenticity proof of the brand.
Anyone can post a flat-lay. Not many people document the actual
experience of thrift sourcing — the failures, the intuitions, the
misses. This content builds the parasocial connection that converts
followers into buyers. It also generates natural, specific, local
language that helps SEO without trying to.

Format: 300-600 words. One or two photos (in-shop if possible).
One header image. Date and location tagged clearly.
Optional: links to any items found on that trip.

Voice: First person as the author.
"I was done. Four hours into Chatuchak on a Sunday..."
Raw, specific, no brand polish. Honest about when trips
produce nothing. This is a journal, not a press release.

SEO value: Local search terms appear naturally.
"Chatuchak Market vintage", "Phuket thrift shopping",
"secondhand finds Bangkok" — these phrases emerge from
honest writing without keyword stuffing.

Target posts for launch:
- One Field Note by May 1 — the Chatuchak trip that produced ITEM-006
  is already a great story (see item-006 story page for the narrative)

### SECTION 3: THE ARCHIVE (EDITORIAL)
**Subtitle: The Archive**
**Status: BUILD THIRD — begin in June 2026**

What it is: Long-form editorial posts. Brand history deep-dives
(Glad News, Michiko Koshino, Spinash), guides to dating vintage
labels, essays on secondhand culture, creative commentary,
digital art posts, tutorials, and eventually custom work showcases.

This section is also where Archive item companion posts live —
longer essays published alongside the item's story page, going
deeper into the brand history and cultural context behind the piece.
NOTE: Companion posts should be published AFTER an item is sold,
or simultaneously with the drop — not before, because if a user
reads a compelling companion post and then the item is already sold,
it creates frustration. Timing is: item drops → item sells →
companion post publishes within 1 week of sale.

Why it matters: This is where OA builds intellectual authority.
Posts like "The History of Glad News and Why It Still Matters"
or "How to Read a Vintage Label: A Field Guide" establish the
brand as genuinely knowledgeable, not just aesthetically curated.
These are the posts that get linked, shared, and rank long-term.

Digital art and creative work:
The founder is also a visual artist. The Archive is where this
lives — digital art posts, process shots, aesthetic essays,
creative commissions (available via DM, not through the site).
These posts expand OA's identity beyond thrift brand into
creative practice — which is accurate and differentiating.

Voice: Mix. Deep-dives and essays use brand voice.
Creative/personal posts use author's voice.

Target first posts (June 2026):
- "Glad News: A Brief History of Harajuku's Most Honest Gothic Brand"
- "How to Date a Vintage Garment Label" (genuinely useful, highly searchable)
- First digital art post — introduce the creative side

### BLOG INDEX PAGE (/blog/index.html)

Displays all posts in a filterable grid.
Filter tabs: All · Circuit · Field Notes · Archive

Each post card contains:
- Section label (Circuit / Field Notes / Archive) with colour coding
- Post title
- One-line description (the "deck" — written by author for each post)
- Date
- Reading time estimate (calculate: word count / 200 = minutes)
- Thumbnail image

Card colour coding:
- Circuit: accent in --blue (#56CCF2)
- Field Notes: accent in --amber (#E8A020)
- Archive: accent in --violet (#C9B8E8)

Filtering: done in vanilla JavaScript, no library needed.
Filter pills toggle a data attribute on cards, JS shows/hides.
No page reload. See blog.js implementation notes below.

### INDIVIDUAL POST PAGES

Each post is a standalone HTML file.
It imports base.css and nav.css from the parent directory.
It has its own inline or imported blog.css for post-specific layout.

Every post page MUST include:

META TAGS (SEO critical — do not skip):
```html
<title>[Post Title] — Ossuary Atelier: The Osteology</title>
<meta name="description" content="[150-160 char description. Specific. No fluff.]">
<meta property="og:title" content="[Post Title] — Ossuary Atelier">
<meta property="og:description" content="[Same as description]">
<meta property="og:image" content="[Full URL to post thumbnail image]">
<meta property="og:type" content="article">
<meta property="og:url" content="[Full URL of this post]">
<meta name="article:published_time" content="[ISO date: 2026-05-01]">
<link rel="canonical" href="[Full URL of this post]">
```

STRUCTURED DATA (JSON-LD — paste in <head>, helps Google):
```html
<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "BlogPosting",
  "headline": "[Post title]",
  "datePublished": "[ISO date]",
  "dateModified": "[ISO date of last edit]",
  "author": {
    "@type": "Organization",
    "name": "Ossuary Atelier"
  },
  "publisher": {
    "@type": "Organization",
    "name": "Ossuary Atelier",
    "url": "https://ossuaryatelier.github.io"
  },
  "description": "[Post description]",
  "image": "[Full URL to post image]"
}
</script>
```

INTERNAL LINKS every post should have:
- At least one link back to /drops.html or a relevant item page
- Link back to /blog/index.html ("← Back to The Osteology")
- Link to /blog/circuit/index.html if the post mentions sourcing
- Link to /contact.html at the end with a CTA

### COMMENT SYSTEM — GISCUS

Giscus uses GitHub Discussions as the backend.
It is free, open source, ad-free, and requires zero server code.
One script tag per page.

SETUP STEPS (do this once):
1. Go to github.com/[your-username]/ossuary-atelier
2. Settings → Features → enable Discussions
3. Go to giscus.app and configure:
   - Repository: your-username/ossuary-atelier
   - Page → Discussion mapping: "pathname" (each page gets own thread)
   - Discussion category: create one called "Blog Comments"
   - Theme: choose "dark" or paste custom theme CSS
   - Input position: top (so comment box appears before existing comments)
   - Reactions: enable
4. Copy the generated script tag

EMBED on each post page (paste before </body>):
```html
<script src="https://giscus.app/client.js"
  data-repo="[username]/ossuary-atelier"
  data-repo-id="[YOUR_REPO_ID]"
  data-category="Blog Comments"
  data-category-id="[YOUR_CATEGORY_ID]"
  data-mapping="pathname"
  data-strict="0"
  data-reactions-enabled="1"
  data-emit-metadata="0"
  data-input-position="top"
  data-theme="dark"
  data-lang="en"
  crossorigin="anonymous"
  async>
</script>
```

NOTE ON LOGIN: Giscus requires a GitHub account to comment.
This is slightly more friction than Google login but:
- No ads (Disqus free tier injects ads, unacceptable for OA)
- No spam bots (GitHub accounts have friction to create)
- Comments are stored in YOUR repository — you own the data
- GitHub login is common among the demographic that finds you via
  a thrift blog with a gothic editorial aesthetic

If Google login becomes essential later: look into utteranc.es
as an alternative, or a paid Disqus tier. For now, Giscus is correct.

To make Giscus feel warmer and less technical: write a short
prompt above the comment box on each page:
"Leave a note. Tell us if you've been to this shop, found something
similar, or just have something to say. We read everything."

This comment prompt should be styled as part of the page,
not left to Giscus's default UI.

### SITEMAP.XML

Create a file called sitemap.xml in the root of the repository.
Update it every time a new post is added.
Format:

```xml
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?>
<urlset xmlns="http://www.sitemaps.org/schemas/sitemap/0.9">
  <url>
    <loc>https://ossuaryatelier.github.io/</loc>
    <lastmod>2026-05-01</lastmod>
    <changefreq>monthly</changefreq>
    <priority>1.0</priority>
  </url>
  <url>
    <loc>https://ossuaryatelier.github.io/blog/index.html</loc>
    <lastmod>2026-05-01</lastmod>
    <changefreq>weekly</changefreq>
    <priority>0.9</priority>
  </url>
  <url>
    <loc>https://ossuaryatelier.github.io/blog/circuit/index.html</loc>
    <lastmod>2026-05-01</lastmod>
    <changefreq>monthly</changefreq>
    <priority>0.8</priority>
  </url>
  <!-- Add each new blog post URL here when published -->
</urlset>
```

Submit to Google Search Console (free):
1. Go to search.google.com/search-console
2. Add property: URL prefix → your GitHub Pages URL
3. Verify ownership by adding a meta tag to index.html <head>
4. Submit sitemap URL under Sitemaps section

## EDITORIAL GUIDELINES

### VOICE GUIDE

Two modes. Know which one you're in before you start writing.

PERSONAL VOICE (Field Notes, item companion posts, creative posts):
- First person singular: "I", "me", "my"
- Past tense for events: "I found it", "I paid", "I turned it over"
- Specific: times, places, prices, names — always named, never vague
- Honest about failure, uncertainty, and not knowing things
- No brand promotion language: no "stunning", "unique", "must-have"
- Allowed to be funny, confused, tired, excited — human

BRAND VOICE (Circuit guides, partnerships, Archive deep-dives):
- First person plural: "we", "our" — or objective third person
- Present tense for facts, past tense for narrative
- Structured and trustworthy — like a good editorial magazine
- Still specific: no vague claims, always cite evidence or experience
- More authoritative, less personal — but never cold

NEVER use in either voice:
- Generic adjectives: beautiful, stunning, amazing, gorgeous, unique
- Seller language: great condition, perfect for, must see
- Vague provenance: vintage vibes, retro feel, old-school
- Hollow sustainability claims: eco-friendly, planet-conscious, green
  (unless specifically and concretely true)

### POST STRUCTURE TEMPLATE

Every post should have these elements, in roughly this order:

1. A strong first sentence or two that makes you want to keep reading.
   Field Notes: drop the reader into the scene.
   Circuit: state exactly what this guide covers and why it matters.
   Archive: make a claim that's interesting enough to argue with.

2. The body — whatever the post is about. No specific length rule.
   Field Notes: 300-600 words. Circuit: 600-1200 words.
   Archive: 800-2000 words. Don't pad. Stop when done.

3. At least one internal link to an OA item, drop, or other page.

4. A closing line that is NOT a call to action. End with something
   true, specific, or slightly unexpected. The CTA comes after,
   separated visually, as a designed element — not shoehorned
   into the last sentence of the prose.

5. A CTA block — linked to drops page, or to Instagram, or both.
   Designed consistently. Not a paragraph of text.

6. The Giscus comment section, with the warm prompt above it.

## EXPANDING BEYOND CLOTHES

The blog should reflect this from day one, not "eventually."
The Archive section is where non-clothing finds and creative work live.
You do not need to have these items in the shop to write about them.

Post types that do not require inventory:
- Digital art process post (show work, offer commission via DM)
- Aesthetic essay (an object you found that you kept, not sold)
- Tutorial (how to identify a vintage label, how to photograph thrift)
- Exhibition or market you visited
- Creative recommendation (a book, a film, a reference that connects
  to the brand's aesthetic — written with the same specificity and
  honesty as everything else)

When non-clothing items do enter the shop:
- They get an item page just like clothing items
- If they are Archive tier, they get a QR tag and story page
- The blog post referencing them is an Archive post
- Website navigation will eventually need a "Objects" category
  alongside "Drops" — but this is a later build, not now

## WHAT A DEVELOPER TAKING OVER NEEDS TO KNOW

If you are a developer or collaborator who did not write the original
code, here is the technical context:

STACK: Pure HTML / CSS / JavaScript. No framework. No build system.
No npm. No React. Files are edited directly and pushed to GitHub.

HOSTING: GitHub Pages. The repository is at
github.com/[username]/ossuary-atelier. Everything in the root and
subdirectories is served as static files. No server-side code.

FONTS: Cinzel (display, 400/600/700/900) and Cormorant Garamond
(body, 300/400/600, italic variants). Loaded from Google Fonts.
Both are present in the <head> of every page.

CSS VARIABLES: Defined in /css/base.css. Use these everywhere.
Never hardcode colour hex values in page-level CSS.
Key variables: --void, --violet, --violet-dim, --bruise, --bruise-mid,
--mid, --faint. Full list in base.css.

BLOG COMMENTS: Giscus. Script tag at the bottom of each post page.
Configuration is in the script tag attributes. The GitHub repository
must have Discussions enabled and a "Blog Comments" category created.
See the Giscus section above for full setup.

IMAGES: Stored in /assets/blog/. Compressed to under 300kb before
upload (squoosh.app). WebP format preferred. Always include alt text.

NEW POST CHECKLIST:
1. Write the HTML file in the correct /blog/[section]/ directory
2. Add meta tags, OG tags, and JSON-LD to <head>
3. Add internal links (at minimum: back to blog index, to drops page)
4. Add Giscus script before </body>
5. Add a card for this post to /blog/index.html
6. Add the URL to sitemap.xml with current date
7. Compress and upload any images
8. Push to GitHub — page is live within 2-3 minutes

## OPEN QUESTIONS / FUTURE DECISIONS

These are things not yet decided. Document the decision here
when it is made.

1. DOMAIN — is the site still on github.io or has eu.org been connected?
   If a custom domain is active, update all canonical URLs and sitemap URLs.

2. GOOGLE SEARCH CONSOLE — has the site been submitted and verified?
   If not, do this before any blog posts go live. Unverified sites take
   longer to index.

3. ANALYTICS — is Google Analytics or a privacy-friendly alternative

   the /drops.html page will need a category filter system.
   Note it here so it does not come as a surprise.

5. GISCUS CUSTOM THEME — the default dark theme is functional but
   not fully on-brand. A custom Giscus theme can be written in CSS
   and passed as a URL to the data-theme attribute. Worth doing when
   there is time — not a launch blocker.

6. ARCHIVE COMPANION POST TIMING — current decision: publish after sale,
   within 1 week. Review this after first 3 Archive sales and adjust
   based on what actually happens.
